Rosie, the Storm, and Failed Rescue

2.) I don't think Sho knew much about Rosie at all. I wanna say Mirei and Rak interacted with her more, but unlike some of the more surly members of the crew, Rosie seemed like she was alright. She just saw someone in danger and went to help.

## Extracted Facts

- Sho's attempt to rescue Rosie was total instinct, not planned heroics.
- Sho saw someone in danger and believed she could do something, so she acted.
- Sho did not know Rosie well.
- Sho believed Mirei and Rak likely interacted with Rosie more than she had.
- Rosie seemed like she was all right, unlike some of the more surly crew.
- Sho's plan was simple and immediate: she thought a rope should do the trick and went for it.
- Sho probably blamed herself somewhat for the failed rescue, but not heavily.
- Sho understood Rosie's loss primarily as an unfortunate accident, unlike other shipboard events that were driven by cruelty or choice.
- Rosie's death did affect Sho's view of the crew: she began trying to sort who was rotten from who was a good person trapped in a bad situation.
- Sho would not become more cautious in the same situation. Given the chance again, she would dive after someone every time.

## Reconstruction Use

Use this to clarify Sho's role in Rosie's failed rescue.

Sho's rescue attempt is part of her reflexive moral pattern: danger appears, someone may die, and she moves before thinking through all consequences. It is not about knowing Rosie deeply or making a calculated decision.

Sho's limited guilt also matters. She distinguishes accidents and failures from deliberate cruelty. Rosie's death hurt, but it did not burden Sho in the same way as Aretta's death, the Man's Promise slaughter, or Wormwood punishments.
