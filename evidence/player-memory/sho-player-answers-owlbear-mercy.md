## Raw Player Answer

```text
Owlbear, Mercy, and Plugg’s Cruelty
1.) I think she originally saw him as another one of Plugg’s goons, but during and after the fight she realized he was another victim and captive, which is what drove her to spare and attempt to befriend him. 
2.) Considering I think she saw Owlbear at first to be one of Plugg’s goons, she was up for the challenge of putting them in their place and showing Plugg she could take him out too. Obviously it didn't play out the way she thought in her head. 
3.) Tactical I think, she wasn't panicked like she was in the bilges, and this was just a run of the mill fight. Exploiting your opponent’s weaknesses to take them down quickly and efficiently makes the most sense. 
4.) I think she both saw that Owlbear was beaten and there was no need to continue, but more than that, I think she saw that he was not the “beast” that he was originally made out to be, and didn’t deserve what she had originally set out to do at the start of the fight. 
5.) A bit of pity for Owlbear himself, he was also a victim, she could see it. But also defiance, because she wasn't gonna do what Plugg wanted her to do. 
6.) Big time, befriending the gentle giant was one of the first positive’s she had since being put on this boat. 
7.) A little protective, a bit fond, and wanting to somehow release him so he could hopefully become more.
```

## Extracted Facts

- Sho initially saw Owlbear as another one of Plugg's goons.
- During and after the fight, Sho realized Owlbear was another victim and captive.
- That realization drove Sho to spare him and attempt to befriend him.
- At the start, Sho was willing to take on the challenge because she thought she could put one of Plugg's people in their place and show Plugg she could take him down too.
- The fight did not play out the way Sho expected in her head.
- Sho's fighting style against Owlbear was tactical, not panicked.
- Unlike the bilges fight, Sho saw this as a run-of-the-mill fight and used efficient weakness-exploitation to end it quickly.
- Sho stopped because Owlbear was beaten and there was no need to continue.
- More importantly, Sho realized Owlbear was not the beast Plugg had made him out to be.
- Owlbear did not deserve what Sho had originally intended at the start of the fight.
- Sparing Owlbear came from both pity and defiance.
- Pity: Owlbear was also a victim, and Sho could see it.
- Defiance: Sho refused to do what Plugg wanted her to do.
- Befriending Owlbear was one of the first positives Sho had after being put on the Wormwood.
- Sho became a little protective, fond of him, and wanted to somehow release him so he could become more than what Plugg made him.

## Reconstruction Use

Use this to clarify that Sho's mercy toward Owlbear was not only abstract restraint.

The Owlbear fight begins with Sho misreading him as Plugg's brute. It changes because she recognizes captivity inside the threat. That makes Owlbear one of Sho's early lessons in distinguishing victimized tools from true malice.

This scene should also be contrasted with the bilges fight. Against Owlbear, Sho was tactical and controlled. In the bilges, she was terrified and in survival panic.
