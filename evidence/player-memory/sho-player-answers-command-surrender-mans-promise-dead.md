## Raw Player Answer

```text
Command, Surrender, and the Man’s Promise Dead
1.) She felt confused and possibly a bit trapped, like somehow they were giving her another task to fail so that they could justify killing her. 
2.) As she understood it, once the ship was taken, and the crew surrendered, no further harm needed to come to anyone. So she promised that if they were to stand down and not put up a fight, no one would be hurt. 
3.) I believe she thinks they did since some of the actions taken were in direct response to her words. I seem to remember two instances, where the crew members of the Man’s Promise ran to a lifeboat and  Rak hit the switch to drop it before it was ready, causing it to crash into another lifeboat and potentially killing people. (I don't remember if they died or were just hurt.) But I also remember the infamous moment where Sho demanded a member of the crew put down their weapon and no harm would come to them, and then Harvus just smashed another member of the crew with a flail who wasn't even looking to attack. 
4.) All the above honestly. Scourge and Plugg made her mad, and was always something she could lash out against, but innocent people were being hurt because the people she was supposed to be in command of were not listening, and were people who were pressganged just like her. She felt responsible for the atrocities, powerless to stop them in that moment, and despite working with them, does NOT trust either Harvus or Rak. 
5.) She blames Rak and Harvus, she blames Harrigan for attacking the other ship in the first place, and she blames herself for not doing more to stop it. 
6.) It did in a way. She realizes if she’s to take charge of anyone, she would need to be powerful enough to do so, and be able to hold people to their actions. 
7.) It’s the big reason she feels guilt. She didn't like the idea of attacking another ship in the first place, but she felt like she at least could stop most of the bloodshed if she just convinced them to surrender, and when she catastrophically failed, she harbors a lot of the guilt of their circumstances on her shoulders.
```

## Extracted Facts

- Sho felt confused and possibly trapped when given the command / strike-team role.
- She suspected the role might be another task designed for her to fail so her death could be justified.
- Sho believed that once the Man's Promise was taken and the crew surrendered, no further harm needed to come to anyone.
- Sho promised that if the Man's Promise crew stood down and did not fight, no one would be hurt.
- Sho believes some deaths or injuries happened in direct response to her words.
- Player memory preserves two important examples:
  - Man's Promise crew ran for a lifeboat and Rak hit the switch / release before it was ready, causing it to crash into another lifeboat and potentially kill or injure people.
  - Sho demanded a Man's Promise crew member put down their weapon and promised no harm would come, then Harvus smashed another crew member with a flail who was not even looking to attack.
- The exact casualties in the lifeboat incident are unresolved.
- Sho felt responsible for the atrocities and powerless to stop them.
- Sho was especially upset because the people not listening to her were press-ganged like her.
- Despite working with them, Sho does **not** trust Rak or Harvus.
- Sho blames Rak and Harvus for their direct actions.
- Sho blames Harrigan for attacking the Man's Promise in the first place.
- Sho blames herself for not doing more to stop the bloodshed.
- The event taught Sho that if she is going to take charge, she must be powerful enough to hold people to their actions.
- This failure is a major source of Sho's guilt.
- Sho did not like the idea of attacking another ship, but believed she could limit bloodshed by securing surrender.
- When that failed catastrophically, she internalized much of the guilt for the Man's Promise dead.

## Reconstruction Use

Use this to sharpen Sho's Man's Promise command failure.

The strike-team role did not feel empowering to her. It felt like a possible trap or test she was expected to fail. Her surrender promise was sincere: surrender should have ended the need for further harm.

The moral wound comes from the promise being broken by people ostensibly under her command. This is why the Man's Promise battle is not only guilt over enemy deaths; it is guilt over failed authority, broken word, and inability to restrain Rak and Harvus.
