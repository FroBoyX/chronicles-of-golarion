## Extracted Facts

- Sho's cloak was primarily comfort and a quiet promise to herself that she would see Agatha again.
- Recovering the cloak was reclaiming part of herself.
- Sho could not lose something that important to her.
- Sho was embarrassed that she did not realize she had been swindled until too late.
- Pride made her reluctant to bring the swindle back up.
- She was still deeply glad to get the cloak back.
- Sho hid the cloak after recovering it.
- She hid it because she could not risk someone taking it or damaging it.
- Refusing / ditching the rum was partly quiet resistance, but also practical: Sho wanted to keep her wits.
- Rum refusal also came from monastic teaching: alcohol as poison of the body and mind.
- Agatha had tried to get Sho to loosen up about alcohol, but old habits persisted, especially in a hostile environment like the Wormwood.
- The rum refusal was more practicality than defiance.
- Sho was afraid of getting caught because she did not want to be forced to drink the rum.

## Reconstruction Use

Use this to deepen Sho's cloak and rum-ration threads.

The cloak should not be treated as only equipment. It is a comfort object, a selfhood anchor, and a promise to Agatha. Losing it threatens continuity with the person Sho is trying to remain.

Rum refusal should not be flattened into pure rebellion. It is mostly practical self-control under danger, with a quiet resistance component and a monastic habit against intoxication.
