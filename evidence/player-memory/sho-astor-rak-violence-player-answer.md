## Raw Player Answers

### 1. Before the Man's Promise assault, did Sho see Rak as dangerous, useful, funny, chaotic, loyal, or something else?

> Before the Man’s Promise she saw him as an oddity. Chaotic, and occasionally funny, there was nothing really hinting at the cold nature of him, so the sudden switch confused and changed her view.

### 2. Did Sho believe Rak understood why surrendered enemies mattered to her?

> I honestly dont think Sho think’s Rak thought about it. Much like a lot of her actions are based on instinct, I think she view’s Rak’s actions like a child doing something he thinks is fun. Which makes him VERY dangerous.

### 3. Was Sho more disturbed by Rak's violence itself, or by his refusal to respect limits once Sho had set them?

> Honestly it's a tie. The fact that he could go from a goofy lizardman to a murderer in the span of a few moments was disturbing, but the fact that he ignored her limits meant that he didn't see her as enough of a threat to keep him from doing what he wanted.

### 4. Did Rak's actions make Sho question whether they could safely stay on the same crew?

> Oh absolutely. There’s a non zero chance that if the Man’s promise crew successfully mutinies and Rak and Harvus aren't on board, she might be fine with leaving them on the island.

### 5. Does Sho believe Rak can be reasoned with?

> I think she’s of the impression that he can, but it requires either a) something that is of interest to Rak. Or b) something vital to Rak’s survival. Appealing to his moral compass is out of the question.

### 6. What would Rak have to do for Sho to decide he must be stopped?

> She’s already considering it. But for a situation of he HAS to be stopped, it’d probably need to be another situation where an innocent person is at risk, and Rak shows no signs of stopping.

### 7. What would Rak have to do for Sho to trust him again?

> He would actually have to show some kind of remorse for his actions. As it stands, he’s seemed to have committed several acts of violence with no shred of remorse. If at any point she could see he felt bad for doing any of those things, there might be a bridge to connect there, but as it stands, he is a means to an end, and once she gets what she needs, he will either be stopped or left behind.

## Extracted Sho-Character Truth

- Before the Man's Promise battle, Sho saw Rak as an oddity: chaotic, occasionally funny, and not clearly cold or murderous.
- The sudden shift from goofy lizardman to murderer confused Sho and permanently changed how she viewed him.
- Sho does not think Rak meaningfully considered why surrendered enemies mattered to her.
- Sho frames Rak's violence as instinctive and almost childlike: doing something he thinks is fun, which makes him extremely dangerous.
- Sho was equally disturbed by Rak's violence and by his refusal to respect the limits she tried to set.
- Rak ignoring Sho's limits made Sho feel he did not see her as enough of a threat to restrain him.
- Sho questions whether she and Rak can safely remain on the same crew.
- If the Man's Promise crew successfully mutinies and Rak and Harvus are not aboard, Sho might be willing to leave them on Bonewrack Isle.
- Sho believes Rak can be reasoned with only through self-interest or survival interest, not moral appeal.
- Sho is already considering that Rak may need to be stopped.
- Sho's hard line is another situation where an innocent person is at risk and Rak shows no sign of stopping.
- For Sho to trust Rak again, she would need to see real remorse.
- Without remorse, Sho sees Rak as a means to an end; once she gets what she needs, he may be stopped or left behind.
