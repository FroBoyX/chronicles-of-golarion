## Raw Player Answer

```text
Harrigan, the Fog, and the Captain’s Reward
Honestly, I’m not sure she knows. I suppose it was more instinct than anything. She didn't think. She just saw it about to happen and warned. I suppose you could throw in a refusal to allow a dishonorable murder though.
This girl almost never thinks about the consequences before acting. She just did it and didn't realize the consequences until after.
Confusion and discomfort I think. I don't think she fully grasped how much her life was on the line in this fight, and I think she was discomforted that not only was her life “spared”, but it was done at the expense of innocent people’s lives.
Luck with a bit of guilt honestly.
I’m not sure she knows what to do with it or how to feel about it. She doesnt want to get rid of it, but her feelings on the pendant are very confused.
I think she sees a distinction between Harrigan and Plugg and Scourge. Harrigan may very well need to be taken down for his actions at some point, but she doesnt at least put him in the same category as Plugg and Scourge, who need to be taken out as soon as possible.
```

## Extracted Facts

- Sho is not fully sure why she warned Harrigan during the Man's Promise battle.
- The warning was mostly instinct: she saw it about to happen and warned before thinking through the consequences.
- A refusal to allow a dishonorable murder may also have been part of the impulse.
- Sho often acts before considering consequences.
- She did not realize the consequences of warning Harrigan until after the moment passed.
- Harrigan's reward produced confusion and discomfort.
- Sho did not fully grasp how much her life was on the line in the Man's Promise fight.
- Sho was uncomfortable that her life was effectively spared at the expense of innocent people's lives.
- Sho reads the reward as luck mixed with guilt.
- Sho does not know what to do with the pendant / captain's amulet or how to feel about it.
- Sho does not want to get rid of the pendant, but her feelings about it are deeply confused.
- Sho distinguishes Harrigan from Plugg and Scourge.
- Harrigan may eventually need to be taken down for his actions, but Sho does not place him in the same category as Plugg and Scourge.
- Plugg and Scourge need to be taken out as soon as possible in Sho's mind.

## Reconstruction Use

Use this to refine Sho's relationship to Harrigan's reward.

The warning is not loyalty to Harrigan. It is instinct, possibly shaped by Sho's refusal to permit dishonorable murder even against a dangerous captain. The reward does not comfort her. It creates moral confusion because her survival and advancement are tied to a battle in which innocent people died.

The captain's amulet / pendant should be treated as emotionally unresolved: Sho does not want to discard it, but it is not a clean badge of pride.
