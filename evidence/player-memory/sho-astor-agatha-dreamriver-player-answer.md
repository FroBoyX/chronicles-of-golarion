## Raw Player Answers

### 1. What did Agatha represent to Sho when they first met?

> Agatha represented kindness, freedom, beauty, and yes a future she had never thought possible before. Prior to meeting her, Sho had expected to just stay at the monastery forever, no real goals, no ideas put in her head that she could even HAVE goals. But meeting Agatha on that fateful day changed so much, someone could be beautiful AND strong, kind and free, inspiring instead of stifling.

### 2. What did Agatha open up in Sho?

> She honestly opened up Sho to the possibilities of the world, and means the world to her.

### 3. When did Sho understand how she felt?

> I don't think she fully understood how she felt until Agatha had already left Jalmery. Like it took the absence of all those feelings to make her realize how much she needed that in her life, and how much she needed to go out in the world to see and experience.

### 4. Did Sho want to find Agatha again?

> She absolutely wanted to find Agatha again. She wanted to become more learned, and impress her with her own knowledge of the world. She also wanted to be with her moving forward, seeing the world together rather than just hearing about it second hand.

### 5. Did Agatha help Sho survive the Wormwood and Man's Promise?

> Absolutely, it gave her a focus to help direct her actions, and a goal to hopefully get to once she could escape the ship.

### 6. Does Sho fear Agatha would be disappointed or disgusted by what Sho has done?

> A bit yeah. Especially the Man’s Promise, she worries that Agatha would be disgusted with her. 
> She absolutely still wants to meet her. She made a promise to herself to do so, so come hell or high water, she’s gonna.

### 7. How does Sho think Agatha might respond if she knew everything?

> I think Agatha would be taken aback a bit, but after thinking things through, and knowing how dangerous the sea could be, I think she would come to understand why Sho took the actions she did. She wouldn't be happy about it, but she would understand.
