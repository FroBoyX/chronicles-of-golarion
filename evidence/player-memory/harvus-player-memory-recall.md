## Raw Player Answers

### Batch 1 — Port Peril / Core Identity / Scourge / Calistria

Question 1: Before the Formidably Maid, how did Harvus see himself in Port Peril: worker, exile, survivor, protector, thug, outsider, or something else?

> Harvus is definitely a survivor. A lifetime a violent bigotry has hardened him. He is a thug in the best of times, a brute when his rage breaks.

Question 2: At the start of the campaign, was Harvus' hatred of humans active, dulled, hidden, easily provoked, or already complicated?

> Easily Provoked. He's not dumb enough to display it brazenly. But if you push him enough, he'll pop.

Question 3: What old loss still mattered most to Harvus before the Wormwood: family, home, village, dignity, safety, freedom, or something else?

> Freedom. I'd say he buried his loss for his family and village with the slavers that took him.

Question 4: When Scourge damaged Harvus' wing, what changed most: pain, humiliation, fear, rage, loss of freedom, or the feeling that Scourge had made it personal?

> I don't think he'd be humiliated by it. It took look 9 people to bring him down. I think that would actually put a little pep in his step. lol I think that knowing Scourge, for whatever reason, (probably mutal racism) made it personal is what is getting to him.

Question 5: When Harvus learned Scourge said he needed to be "tamed," did that make Scourge the true target immediately, or did that build over time?

> Scourge was the true target as soon as he learned who was responsible for his wing. That shmuck Maheem might have "pulled the trigger" but Scourge gave the order.

Question 6: For Harvus, was Calistria mainly faith, comfort, revenge, discipline, permission, habit, or a private bargain?

> He fell into Calistrian faith purely because it promotes revenge. I don't think he's a pious man, he doesn't preach, nor does he do a lot of praying. Unless someone has crossed him, then he might should word her way to look out for him as plots recompense on someone.

Question 7: In early Wormwood, what did "proper revenge" against Scourge mean to Harvus: death, humiliation, confession, wing-for-wing repayment, poetic justice, pirate judgment, or something else?

> I would say "Wing-for-wing" but I don't want to say what I have planned per se, IF I get my hands on him. I would like to leave that a surprise. Unless you think you might need to know.

## Extracted Player Truth

### Core Identity

- Harvus sees himself as a survivor hardened by violent bigotry.
- He can be a thug in ordinary circumstances and a brute when rage breaks.
- His hatred of humans is easily provoked, but not openly displayed by default.
- The active pre-campaign loss is freedom.
- Family and village grief exists, but Harvus considers it buried with the slavers who took them.

### Scourge and the Wing

- The wing injury did not primarily humiliate Harvus.
- Harvus takes some pride in the fact that many people were required to bring him down.
- What mattered was that Scourge made the injury personal.
- Scourge became the true target as soon as Harvus learned Scourge was responsible for the wing injury.
- Harvus assigns primary responsibility to Scourge as the order-giver, even if Maheem or others helped carry out the press-ganging.

### Calistria and Revenge

- Harvus is not broadly pious.
- He was drawn to Calistria because Calistria promotes or sanctifies revenge.
- He does not preach and does not pray often.
- He may invoke Calistria when someone has crossed him and he is plotting recompense.
- Desired revenge against Scourge is player-confirmed as "wing-for-wing" in broad terms.
- Exact revenge plan is intentionally withheld by the player and should remain undisclosed unless future GM prep requires it.

### Sandara

- Harvus noticed Sandara's healing first.
- His early courtly affection was playful survival behavior mixed with some Calistrian habit.
- He could take stock of Sandara's beauty and be interested in a dalliance, but her being human limited his trust.
- Sandara's help with the wing likely made Harvus feel pitied and exposed.
- Harvus was skeptical of unconditional help from any human.

### Grok

- Player does not currently remember what Harvus got Grok to help with.
- Archive should preserve the question as a memory gap and use existing notes/logs to reconstruct Grok's exact practical role.

### Rosie

- Harvus returned Rosie's fiddle primarily as a way to make an ally.
- Rosie was probably not a friend in a warm/intimate sense from Harvus' point of view.
- She became a dependable ally he could trust.
- Rosie's loss became rage, especially because Harvus was useless in trying to save her.

### Sho

- Harvus viewed Sho as a menace, mostly to herself.
- He also saw her as capable and potentially useful.
- He dismissed Sho's discomfort with Man's Promise boarding violence.
- Harvus' logic was that violence is sink-or-swim on the high seas.
- Learning Scourge tried to have Sho killed or boxed made Harvus think Sho was foolish, and also that Scourge was foolish in how he treated crew.
- This reinforced Harvus' contempt for rule-through-fear leadership.

### Rak

- Harvus saw Rak as useful, funny, dependable, and likely the PC he enjoyed and trusted most.
- Rak giving Harvus Sandara's holy symbol reads, in player memory, as a sign of trust.
- Player speculates Rak may have understood Harvus was pursuing Sandara and gave him the heads-up/tool because Harvus had backed Rak before.

### Mirei

- Before Bonewrack, Harvus did not think much of Mirei.
- He likely saw her as a pretty face and a good voice.
- Player doubts Harvus knows Mirei is an aasimar.
- Mirei returning to Riptide Cove with Harvus dramatically changed his view of her.
- Harvus sees her as someone who walked into a monster den with a half-dead birdman on a potential suicide mission and helped him survive.

### Violence and Boarding Logic

- During the Man's Promise boarding, Harvus understood killing as survival.
- He believed failing to fight could lead to punishment, keelhauling, or death.
- He sees killing in boarding action as work and probably as what pirates do.
- He does not primarily frame boarding-action killing as murder.

### Crew Logic

- Harvus views crew practically: crew is crew.
- Liking each other is not required.
- Trust is not automatic.
- Working together is necessary because failure to do so gets people hurt or killed.
- Someone can become crew by working with Harvus, regardless of circumstance.
- Surrendered enemies can become future crew, but trust must be earned.
- Surrender under threat or coercion creates more suspicion.
- Unconditional surrender still creates suspicion, but allows more room for future trust.
- Harvus wants to be feared, but not to build loyalty through fear.
- He sees fear-based loyalty as weak, purchasable, and easily discarded.
- Harvus most wants to be left alone, but understands that respect helps keep someone alive.
