## Raw Player Answers

### 1. What did Sho think of Giffer before the Man's Promise assault?

> I believe Sho saw Giffer as just another victim of being forced to be here on the Wyrmwood.

### 2. Did Sho feel responsible for Giffer because Giffer was under her command?

> She did! Not only was she under Sho’s command, but if memory serves, Sho had stabilized Giffer before moving on to do more on the Man’s Promise. Which is why her death causes suspicion and guilt.

### 3. After the battle, what did Sho know for certain about Giffer's fate?

> She knew that Giffer had been stable the last she saw her, and that upon returning she had died, with no obvious sign as to the cause.

### 4. Did Sho suspect Rak, suspect the chaos of battle, or simply not know what happened?

> As I recall, Sho did not find any evidence to lead her to any specific conclusion. She suspects foul play, but I dont know if that’s due to her really feeling like that, or guilt that she might have not done as good a job in addressing Giffer’s wounds before leaving.

### 5. Did not knowing make the guilt worse?

> Absolutely. It meant no justice for Giffer, another meaningless death.

### 6. If Sho later learned Rak killed Giffer, would that change how she sees Rak, or only confirm what she already feared?

> Oh 100%. She doesnt trust Rak already, and if she found this out, it’d be one more reason she would need to take him down in the future.

## Extracted Player-Memory Claims

- Sho saw Giffer as another victim of being forced aboard the Wormwood, not as an enemy or disposable crew member.
- Sho felt responsible for Giffer because Giffer was under Sho's command during the Man's Promise assault.
- Player memory says Sho stabilized Giffer before moving on during the battle.
- Sho's certain character knowledge was that Giffer had been stable the last time Sho saw her, then later died without an obvious cause.
- Sho did not find evidence leading to a specific culprit.
- Sho suspected foul play, but the player is unsure whether that suspicion came from genuine observed evidence or from guilt over possibly failing to treat Giffer well enough.
- The lack of proof made Sho's guilt worse because it meant no justice for Giffer and made Giffer's death feel meaningless.
- If Sho later learned Rak killed Giffer, it would intensify her existing distrust of Rak and become another reason she believes she may need to take him down in the future.
