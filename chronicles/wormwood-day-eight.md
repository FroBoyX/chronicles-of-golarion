## Narrative Log

The rain worsened during the night.

By dawn, the Wormwood had lost the horizon.

Sea and sky had closed together beyond the rails, both the same hard gray beneath the storm. Rain crossed the deck sideways. Wind caught every loose edge of canvas and drove water through clothing before anyone had finished climbing from the hold.

The bell called all hands.

Day watch came up. Night watch stayed.

There was no ordinary work after that. The ship had one task, and every person aboard became part of it.

The deck rolled underfoot with enough force to throw sailors against rails, masts, and one another. Water swept across the boards faster than it could be cleared. Lines went taut, slackened, then snapped tight again as the sails strained above. Orders vanished into the wind unless they were shouted from only a few feet away.

Peppery Longfarthing stood high on the poop deck where the rain struck hardest.

She did not look comfortable. Nobody did. Her coat clung to her, her hair was plastered back, and both hands stayed occupied with the ship. One held whatever purchase the rail offered. The other cut through the storm with signals and corrections that kept the helm, the riggers, and the deck crews working from the same intention.

The sea ignored her calculations whenever it pleased.

Peppery calculated again.

At moments, pale light moved near her hand or flashed along a line where the spray had swallowed it. No one below had time to name what she was doing. They only knew that a fouled rope became visible, an order reached farther than it should have, or a sailor found the next handhold before the deck dropped away.

Harrigan remained above with her. Plugg carried his orders down. Scourge drove them across the main deck.

None of them could command the storm.

They could only keep the Wormwood answering it.

Harvus went aloft with the other riggers.

The mast moved beneath him while the rigging jerked in his hands. Rain flattened his feathers and ran into his eyes. His injured wing stayed tight against his body where it could, but every climb and reach pulled at the damage Scourge had left him.

There was no room to favor it.

The sailors above the deck worked by touch as often as sight. Wet rope burned across palms. Canvas cracked close enough to sound like cannon fire. Each time the Wormwood dropped into a trough, the rigging seemed to fall with it before the next rise dragged everything upward again.

A violent roll tore Harvus from one foothold.

Maheem caught the back of his harness before the sea could take the mistake farther. Harvus slammed against the mast, found the rope again, and kept working. Neither man had breath to make anything of it. A moment later Harvus braced a line while Maheem crossed above him.

Whatever stood between them could wait for weather that allowed grudges.

Below, the storm entered wherever the ship gave it an opening.

Water ran down ladders and through seams. The middle hold smelled of wet wood, frightened animals, damp clothing, and bodies packed into too little space. Crates shifted against their lashings. Hammocks swung hard enough to strike the supports. Every trip through the passageways required one hand for the ship and another for whatever work could still be carried.

Mirei worked with the lower crew while the storm hammered the deck above. There was no place for the harp in her hands and no quiet corner for a song. The ship needed water moved, passageways cleared, and anything loose secured before it became another danger.

When the deck tilted under her, Slippery Syl caught Mirei by the upper arm and drove her against a support instead of letting her slide toward the hatchway. Syl released her as soon as Mirei had footing. No thanks passed between them. The next bucket was already coming.

Sho worked wherever another pair of hands was needed.

The storm gave her nothing clean to oppose. There was no officer standing still long enough to challenge and no enemy she could put down. There was only the next line, the next bucket, and the next sailor trying not to lose footing.

Once, she and Fipps Chumlett ended up shoulder to shoulder against a crate that had torn halfway free. Fipps cursed her, the crate, and the sea with equal force. Sho drove her weight against the wood while he fought the lashing back through the ring. When it held, they separated without looking at one another.

Rak's day narrowed to practical things.

The galley could not stop feeding the crew, even when every surface pitched and anything left unsecured tried to cross the room. Kroop remained upright through habit, luck, and whatever he had already been drinking. Rak tied down pots, caught knives before they became loose steel, and turned whatever could still be heated into broth thick enough to keep exhausted sailors moving.

The galley fire failed twice.

Rak brought it back twice.

When other work pulled him away, he carried, braced, tied down, and returned to whatever had shifted in his absence. Grok opened stores without argument when the ship needed rope, canvas, or lamp oil. Riaris Krine left the guns long enough to help secure anything heavy enough to kill someone when the Wormwood rolled.

Habbly Quarne moved between damaged hands and damaged wood. He wrapped split palms, set one sailor's shoulder, then left the man holding a brace while he inspected a seam that had begun taking water.

The Wormwood survived because nobody aboard was allowed to be only one thing.

The day watch worked through its hours.

The night watch never truly left.

Patch Patchsalt appeared on deck long before darkness, already dressed for the watch that should have belonged to her later. Tam Tate, Samms, Ratline, Conchobhar, Maheem, Syl, and Harvus rotated through the rigging until rotation meant only that one pair of ruined hands replaced another.

Below, Sandara moved between bailing crews and anyone who had fallen hard enough to need help standing. Mirei, Sho, Badger, Rosie, Shivikah, Jack, Giffer, Crimson, Aretta, Jape, and Fipps kept water and cargo moving because stopping either meant letting the storm choose what broke next.

Plugg and Scourge drove the labor without allowing the weather to become an excuse.

Fear counted as hesitation. Exhaustion counted as slowness. Anyone who stopped long enough to look overwhelmed found an order waiting for them.

Yet command alone was not enough.

Scourge hauled when a line needed another body. Plugg went to the rail when the deck crew could not hear him from above. Even sailors who hated them took the same rope when the pull came, because the line did not care who had given the order.

The storm did not care who had been punished, who had earned favor, or who had begun making friends. It struck the officers' deck and the lower hold with the same rain. The difference was that some sailors could order others into it.

Hours lost their edges.

Morning became afternoon without the sky growing brighter. Meals were swallowed where the crew could find a moment. Rest came in scraps too short to dry clothing or stop hands from shaking. Sailors crouched against bulkheads with bowls in their laps, closed their eyes for minutes, then went back when another name was called.

The first twelve hours felt endless.

The next eight barely felt separate from them.

Day watch became night work. Night watch became whatever labor remained after the lamps were lit. There was no handoff, only bodies changing places while the Wormwood climbed another wall of water.

Darkness changed the storm without lessening it.

Lanterns gave the rain a narrow glow and made the water on deck shine before it ran away into blackness. The ship drove onward through a sea no one could see beyond the next rise. Wind tore words apart. Waves struck hard enough to shake the lower hold.

There was no performance below. No gathered audience. No room for the small freedoms the crew had begun claiming after work.

Only the storm remained.

Rosie went overboard sometime after night had erased the last difference between sea and sky.

Harvus saw her first.

From the rigging, he caught the brief sight of a small body in the black water astern. Rosie appeared on the crest of one wave, vanished behind another, then surfaced again far enough away that the distance seemed impossible.

His warning reached the deck in fragments, but her name survived the wind.

Rak seized a line.

Sho was already knotting another around herself while he made the throwing coil. There was no long argument. Someone was in the water. A rope should have been enough, but the distance was opening too quickly to trust the rope alone.

Sho secured the second line around her waist and put its other end into Rak's hands. He looped it around a belaying point and shoved it toward the sailors gathering behind him. Then Sho went over the rail as Rak made the first throw.

The sea took her immediately.

Cold water closed over Sho's head. The Wormwood surged forward before she could settle into a stroke. Her tether snapped taut, dragged her through the wake, then slackened just enough for her to fight toward the place Rosie had risen.

At the same time, Rak's first cast struck the water short of Rosie, vanished in foam, and drew past as the Wormwood continued forward.

They hauled it in and threw again.

Sho swam for her life while the line passed overhead and across the water beyond her. She gained a few strokes whenever the ship slowed against a wave, then lost them when the hull drove down the other side. Water filled her mouth. The rain struck the sea so hard that the surface seemed to boil around her.

The next cast reached Rosie.

She caught it.

For one breath, the line came taut between the ship and the sea. Sailors shouted. Rak braced. Sho fought toward the place where Rosie clung.

Then Rosie disappeared behind the next swell.

When the line rose again, her hands were no longer on it.

They tried again.

The Wormwood was moving too fast to wait gently for either woman. Every wave changed the distance. Every moment the throwing line spent in the water pulled it farther from where Rosie had been. Sho's tether did the opposite, dragging her wherever the ship went no matter how hard she swam against it.

Once Rosie caught the line and lost it almost immediately. Another time her hand seemed to close before the sea rolled her away. Sho saw her on the crest of a wave, fought toward her, and watched the distance open again when the Wormwood surged forward.

Frustration became desperation.

Mirei fixed her attention on the small figure beyond the wake.

The magic did not come to her as a formula chosen from a list. It came as reach. As need. As the refusal to accept that the wind should decide whether Rosie heard her.

Mirei willed her voice across the storm.

Hold on. We are trying to get you.

Tie the rope around yourself.

When it reaches you again, tie it fast.

There was no answer.

Mirei could not know whether Rosie heard her, only that the words left her and crossed a distance no ordinary voice could survive.

Rak threw again.

Sho kept swimming.

Rosie rose once more, farther back now. The line touched the water near her. Sho drove herself toward the same place while her tether pulled hard against her waist.

Then the Wormwood changed direction under Peppery's command, answering a wave that would have taken the deck broadside.

The turn saved the ship.

It swept the throwing line away from Rosie and pulled Sho sideways through the wake. Her next stroke found nothing where Rosie had been. The tether came taut with the full weight of the racing Wormwood behind it and dragged Sho away before she could correct herself.

The chance to reach Rosie broke apart.

The rescue changed.

If they did not recover Sho now, the ship would take her too.

Rak wrapped her tether around the belaying point and hauled.

Mirei seized it beside him. Sandara and Maheem joined. Then more hands closed behind them, sailors from both watches pulling on the same wet rope while the Wormwood dragged Sho through the dark.

The line cut into palms. It jerked hard enough to pull the front rank forward. Rak held the anchor point while Mirei hauled hand over hand, every gain threatened by the next roll.

Sho struck the side of the hull and vanished beneath spray.

They kept pulling.

When her hands reached the rail, Rak and Mirei caught what they could. Others took her arms, clothing, and the line around her waist. Together they dragged her over the side and onto the deck.

Sho came aboard coughing seawater, bruised by the hull, and furious that she had come back alone.

Everyone looked astern.

Rosie was gone from view.

They watched one wave. Then another. Harvus searched from above until rain and darkness gave him nothing more to find. Rak held the first rescue line as it trailed empty behind the ship. Mirei reached again with instinct and will, sending words toward a place where Rosie might still have been.

No answer came back.

Rosie Cusswell was lost at sea.

There was no body. No proof of death. Only the storm, the distance opening behind the Wormwood, and the knowledge that another ship would have to find her if she had survived.

The crew had no time to decide what that meant.

A line forward had begun to fail.

Peppery's order cut through the deck. Scourge drove hands toward the rigging. Someone pulled Sho away from the rail and put a bucket into her grasp once she could stand. Rak coiled what remained of the rescue line and went where the next pull needed weight. Mirei stayed near Sho long enough to make sure she was breathing, then returned to the labor with Rosie's silence still inside her.

Harvus remained aloft.

Below, the fiddle waited where Rosie had left it.

The watches continued.

No one dried. No one slept long enough to dream. Nobody gathered to speak Rosie's name without the wind taking it from them.

The Wormwood survived the day.

The day did not end when Rosie disappeared.

The storm did not leave with the light.

There was still no dry place, and the crew kept working.
