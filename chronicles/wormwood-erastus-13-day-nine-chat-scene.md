## Narrative Chat Scene

The storm loosened its grip by degrees.

Morning did not arrive cleanly. The sky remained low and gray, the rain still drove across the deck, and the Wormwood continued to rise hard beneath every swell. But the waves no longer struck from every direction at once. The wind left spaces between its blows. Orders began reaching farther than the length of an arm.

For the first time since the bell had called all hands, the crew could tell that the weather was weakening.

That did not mean the work was finished.

Day watch had come up the morning before. Night watch had never gone below. Both remained on their feet while Peppery Longfarthing judged the sea, the canvas, and the strength left in the people answering her signals.

She released them in pieces.

The most exhausted hands went first, though even they did not go directly to sleep. Wet clothing had to be stripped away. Cuts needed wrapping. Hammocks and blankets had to be found beneath everything the storm had soaked or shifted. Some sailors reached the lower hold and sat against the nearest support because climbing into a hammock required more effort than they had left.

Others remained above.

The storm had spent a day trying to take the Wormwood apart. Now the crew had to learn what it had loosened.

Harvus climbed the rigging again.

The work was slower than the day before, but no easier on his body. Every line had to be checked by hand. Knots swollen with rain resisted cold fingers. Canvas needed inspecting where the wind had strained it, and the mast demanded attention wherever rope had rubbed, snapped tight, or shifted under load.

His injured wing had stiffened through the night. He kept it close and worked around the pain.

Whenever the rigging gave him a clear view astern, he looked.

The sea behind the Wormwood held broken foam, rain, and the long wake of a ship that had not stopped moving.

It held no sign of Rosie.

Below, standing water had to be cleared from places where it had collected faster than the pumps and buckets could move it. Lashings were retied. Barrels were opened and checked. Anything spoiled by seawater was separated from whatever could still be saved.

Grok counted rope, lamp oil, canvas, and damaged stores with a face that discouraged interruption. Habbly Quarne moved between split hands, swollen joints, bruised ribs, and the places where the ship itself had taken hurt. He treated each according to how quickly it needed to return to work.

Plugg and Scourge restored ordinary discipline before ordinary strength returned.

The storm had allowed no room for idleness. Recovery allowed even less. Anyone who looked toward a hammock before being dismissed found another task. Anyone who moved too slowly was reminded that the ship had survived because work had continued.

Peppery did not need the reminder repeated. She kept the helm and rigging crews moving through corrections that had become quieter without becoming less exact. Harrigan remained near command, watching the sea open around his ship again.

The Wormwood had survived.

That was the only judgment the officers seemed interested in making.

Rak returned to the galley.

The room looked as though the storm had searched it for anything poorly secured and punished every mistake it found. Pots had shifted despite their lashings. Water had worked beneath doors and around seams. The fire needed coaxing rather than rescuing now, but the crew needed heat more than ever.

Rak and Kroop made food that could be swallowed by people too tired to care what it was.

Broth went up by the pot. Bread that had remained dry was divided without ceremony. Sailors came through carrying bowls in both hands because one hand alone could not be trusted to stop shaking.

Rak kept working.

The rescue lines had left their marks across his palms. Sho's tether had burned through skin where he had anchored and hauled. Every time he closed his hands around a handle, rope, or ladle, the damage reminded him how close the sea had come to taking two people instead of one.

Sho worked through worse.

The line around her waist had left dark bruising. Her arms and shoulders protested every lift. The side of her body carried the places where the Wormwood's hull had struck her while the crew dragged her home.

None of it made her regret going over the rail.

Rosie had been in the water. Sho had believed she could reach her. Given the same moment again, she would have tied the rope and jumped again.

That certainty did not stop her from replaying the distance.

A stronger stroke. A better angle. One less wave between them.

The thoughts came and went while she carried buckets and helped restore the lower deck. She did not let them become confession. The storm had taken Rosie, not Sho's decision to try.

Mirei moved through the same work more quietly than before.

During the rescue, she had reached beyond shouting. She had willed her voice toward Rosie through wind and rain, telling her to hold on and tie the rope fast.

Nothing had answered.

Now ordinary sounds returned to the ship one by one. Boots on wet boards. Axes and hammers working through repair. Groans from sailors waking after too little sleep. The creak of hammocks settling back into their usual motion.

Mirei listened among them without meaning to.

There was still no answer.

Rosie's place in the lower hold remained.

Her hammock hung among the others. Her fiddle waited where she had left it, returned to her only the night before the storm took her away. No officer gathered the crew to speak her name. No bell marked the loss. The Wormwood continued because it had been built to continue without asking whether everyone aboard came with it.

People noticed anyway.

Conchobhar looked toward the empty place more than once. Badger lowered her voice near it. Sandara paused when she passed the fiddle, then went on because someone else needed her hands.

The crew spoke of Rosie in fragments while they worked.

Some remembered the insults. Some remembered the drinking. Some remembered how loudly she had thanked Harvus when he returned the instrument. Others remembered only the black water and the rope slipping from her hands.

By afternoon, the rain had weakened to something that fell mostly downward.

The sea remained rough, but the Wormwood no longer seemed one mistake from disappearing beneath it. The watches separated again. Patch Patchsalt and the night hands were finally given leave to sleep. Day workers returned to recognizable assignments, though few had strength enough to make the return feel normal.

The first true evening after the storm came without celebration.

Nobody asked Mirei to perform. Nobody began a drinking game. Conversations stayed low and short, shaped by exhaustion and the empty hammock among them.

Harvus found Sandara after the worst of the recovery work had ended.

He called her his queen and asked for her aid.

> "My queen, I seek your aid."

Sandara smiled, but the expression shook at the edges. She invited him to sit.

> "A queen now, am I? What can I do for you, Harvus?"

Her attention lingered on the damage he still carried from the storm and the days before it.

> "Not enough to matter tonight... it will have to wait. This is becoming a problem."

Harvus told her about the anger growing inside him.

> "I feel a great swell of anger growing in my heart. Being with you eases my soul. I lost my family when I was young, and a rage burned in my heart for many, many winters. Eventually the pain dulled, but I now know that it was not lost. Scourge rending my wing, Rosie's death last night... I fear being on this forsaken ship has reawakened it."

He had known it before the Wormwood. It had begun when he lost his family young and had burned for many winters before time dulled it. He had believed the rage gone. Scourge tearing his wing had stirred it. Rosie's loss the night before had brought it closer to the surface.

The ship was waking it again.

Harvus told Sandara that being near her eased his soul.

She placed a hand on his shoulder and asked about the wing.

> "Is the wing any better?"

Then she gave him advice that offered no promise of peace.

> "I fear you'll want that fear, Harvus. Keep it... we may have to use it. Things are getting worse on the Wormwood. Rosie was new here, but she angered Scourge and Plugg pretty quick. I was busy in the rigging during the storm... but her going over, it might have been intentional."

Keep the anger, but keep it contained. Things aboard the Wormwood were getting worse. There might come a time when they needed what Harvus was holding inside him.

Sandara had been occupied in the rigging when Rosie went overboard. She had not seen how the fall began. Even so, she remembered how quickly Rosie had angered Scourge and Plugg.

She wondered aloud whether the loss had been entirely accidental.

It was suspicion, nothing more. The storm had been strong enough to take anyone. There was no witness to a hand at Rosie's back and no proof that either officer had arranged what happened.

The thought remained between them anyway.

Sandara told Harvus to keep his rage inside and his friends close. She would pray for Besmara to watch over them.

> "Me and Scourge don't see eye to eye. Keep your rage inside, and your friends close. There may be a time we need to use it. For now, rest your heart, and your wing. I'll pray for Besmara to look over us."

Harvus told her she was the vessel restraining what had begun to rise in him.

> "My, I sully mine spirits to place such a burden on you, but I feel you are the vessel which restrains my rage, my queen."

Sandara accepted the burden in the practical way she accepted most things. If the rage had to remain, she would help him put it to use when the time came.

> "Then I shall attempt to see it put to good use when the time comes. Just call me Sandara, though. Queen is just a little too formal for this gal, okay?"

She asked him to call her Sandara instead of queen.

Then another call came from the deck, and she went back to work.

Harvus watched her go.

> "Good night, Sandara."

Harvus remained behind for a moment.

Above him, the storm was finally moving away.

Below, Rosie's fiddle waited in the dark.

The rain weakened. The watches returned. The Wormwood repaired what the sea had loosened and continued on its course.

By night, the ship had begun to settle back into its old rhythms.

Rosie's hammock stayed empty.
