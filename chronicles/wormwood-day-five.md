**Something in the Bilges**

## Narrative Log

The Wormwood did not soften after punishment.

By morning, the lash had already become part of the ship's memory. Rak and Sho carried it in the way the crew looked at them, then looked away. Some hands watched with interest. Some with warning. Some with the practiced blankness of people who had learned that sympathy could be seen from above.

Harvus had not been tied to the mast the day before, but that did not make him unmarked. Scourge's attention moved through the new hands like a hook dragging through dark water. It caught on whoever seemed useful to hurt next.

The day's problem had already started below.

Jack Scrimshaw had come up from rat-catching pale and shaken, with the look of a boy who had seen the dark move wrong. Something in the bilges had bitten him. Not an ordinary ship rat. Not something small enough to laugh away. The officers had not made much of it at first. There was no profit in admitting that the lowest part of the ship had started biting back.

But a problem ignored was still a problem, and Plugg knew how to turn a problem into work for someone else.

By midmorning, the matter had changed shape. It was no longer Jack's fear. It was no longer a bad corner of the bilges. It was an opportunity.

Rak had already been told he was likely headed below, and now Grok's warning made better sense. The ship had been carrying the order before the order was spoken. Somewhere between the quartermaster's hatch, the galley heat, and the main deck's clean cruelty, the decision had found him.

Plugg chose Rak first.

The cook's mate could go down and see what had come out of the water. If the bilges had a vermin problem, the well-fed lizard could make himself useful. If he came back bitten, perhaps he would remember the rum better next time.

Sho was next.

She had asked why in front of the crew. She had made the officers answer her with more than a lash. The bilges were a quieter place to continue the lesson. Down there, questions could drown in heat and stink before they reached the deck.

Scourge added Harvus.

There was no need to dress it up. He wanted another new hand sent below, another body in the dark, another chance for the ship to grind someone useful against something foul. Harvus was given to the task because Scourge liked the shape of it.

So the three of them went down.

The middle hold swallowed the light by degrees. The air thickened around them as they passed through the animal stink and stored cargo, past the places where the ship kept what it needed and what it wanted forgotten. Heat gathered low and would not move. It lay in the boards, in the walls, in the breath between bodies.

Then came the bilges.

The lowest part of the Wormwood was not merely dirty. It was a place where filth had settled into ownership. Brackish water glimmered under low light. The stink of rot rose from it in layers: old salt, wet wood, spoiled grain, stagnant water, and the sour bite of something living too long where it should have died.

The barley was easy to find.

The captain had taken it aboard cheap in Port Peril, and the Wormwood had gotten what it paid for. Sacks had split in the damp. Grain had swollen and soured. Something had gnawed its way through the consignment and turned the mess into a nest.

The water moved.

Not with the ship.

The first rat came from under the surface like a thrown knife, all wet fur and yellow teeth. It was too large for the word rat to feel reasonable. It hit low and fast, biting before there was room to breathe. Another shape rippled behind it. Then another.

Six of them lived in the bilges.

They did not come all at once in a clean line. They came out of shadow, out of floating chaff, out from under boards slick with bilge water. The cramped space made every movement worse. There was no room to circle. No room for pride. No room for the kind of fight anyone would choose on deck.

Rak had bulk enough to shove into the dark, but the water stole footing and the rats knew the hidden places. Teeth snapped at his legs and found scale where they could. When he drove one back, another thrashed at the edge of sight.

Sho fought smaller and sharper. The bilges did not give her the clean anger of an open challenge or the clear line of a fair opponent. Everything here was wet, close, and wrong. Rats came from beneath, not ahead. They bit and vanished. They made the dark itself feel hostile.

Harvus had height and wings in a place built to deny both. The low beams and sour air pressed him down until the fight became ugly and immediate. He could not be what the open sky made him. He had to be what the bilges allowed: hands, teeth clenched, breath held against the stink, striking at movement before it disappeared.

The rats were not clever in any human way. They were worse than that. They were hungry, hidden, and certain of their own ground. One clung long enough to draw blood. Another vanished beneath the water and came up behind them. A third died thrashing so hard that rotten barley and blood sprayed across the boards.

The fight became sound more than sight.

Water slapped wood. Claws scraped. Bodies struck the low supports. Breath came harsh in the heat. Somewhere above them the ship continued as if nothing important was happening below, as if the Wormwood had not sent three of its newest troublemakers into the dark to solve a danger it had chosen not to fear.

One by one, the rats died.

Not cleanly. Not heroically. They died under boots, against boards, in the water they had used for cover. The last one tried to vanish back into the spoiled barley, but there was nowhere left for it to make the bilges safe for itself again.

When it was over, six bodies floated or lay half-submerged among the ruined grain.

The three new hands stood in the heat with bite marks, blood, sweat, and bilge filth on them. The air still stank. The water still moved with the ship. Nothing about the place felt conquered. Only occupied for one breath longer by the living.

They came back up changed in the way the Wormwood liked to change people: dirtier, angrier, and useful.

Plugg had lost nothing. If they had failed, the rats would have been their fault. If they had taken too long, time would have become another reason for the lash. If they had died badly enough, the ship would have found another way to use the story. Since they had won, the bilges were safer and the officers could call it discipline well spent.

Rak, Sho, and Harvus had killed what Jack had found below.

The Wormwood had found something too.

It had learned that troublemakers could be sent out of sight, into heat and rot and biting dark, and still climb back up with blood on them.

That did not make them safer.

It only made the ship more interested in what else they might survive.
