**Something in the Bilges**

## Narrative Log

The Wormwood did not soften after punishment.

By morning, the lash had already entered the ship's memory. Rak and Sho carried it in the way the crew looked at them, then looked away. Some hands watched with interest. Some with warning. Others had learned to keep their faces empty whenever an officer might be looking down.

Harvus had escaped the mast, but not Scourge's notice.

Yesterday, before the punishment and the music that followed it, Jack Scrimshaw had come up from the bilges frightened and bitten. He had been sent below to catch ordinary rats. What found him in the dark had been much larger.

The officers kept the matter quiet. Jack's fear was easier to dismiss than a danger in the lowest part of the ship, and nobody in command wanted the crew dwelling on something moving beneath their feet.

Grok had heard enough to warn Rak that bilge duty was coming. At the time, she had not known when, or why he would be chosen. By the next morning, Plugg had found his answer.

A hidden problem could be made useful.

Plugg selected Rak first. The cook's mate had failed to drink his rum, answered punishment with insolence, and survived the lash without giving Scourge what he wanted. Now he could make himself useful where the ship was hottest and foulest.

Sho followed. She had questioned Scourge before the assembled crew and forced Plugg to come down from the upper deck to settle it. The bilges offered a quieter continuation of that lesson.

Scourge added Harvus before the order was finished. He did it quickly, with the eagerness of a man who had spotted room for one more name on a punishment detail.

Rak, Sho, and Harvus were told to go below and kill whatever had bitten Jack. They were not to return until it was dead.

The descent carried them through the middle hold, past livestock, stores, and the close traffic of the working ship. Each level trapped more heat. The air grew damp and heavy, thick with animal stink, spoiled food, sweat, and old timber.

At the bottom waited the bilges.

Brackish water covered the lowest boards. It rolled sluggishly with the ship, carrying grease, splintered wood, floating husks, and things better left unidentified. The heat had nowhere to escape. Every breath tasted wet and rotten.

The barley consignment had split open in the damp. Harrigan had bought it cheaply in Port Peril, and the Wormwood had received the bargain it deserved. Sacks sagged against one another, gnawed through and swollen with foul water. Grain had spilled into the bilges and soured there.

Something moved beneath it.

The first dire rat came out of the water in a rush of wet fur and teeth. It struck low, bit, and vanished beneath the floating barley before there was room to answer it.

Then the rest came.

Six dire rats had made the ruined grain their nest. They were broad-backed, filthy, and large enough to turn the cramped bilges into their own ground. They surfaced between the three new hands, scrambled over half-sunken boards, and disappeared into water too dark to follow with the eye.

There was no room to fight properly.

Rak could not bring his full weight to bear without striking a beam or losing his footing. Sho had nowhere to retreat and nowhere to circle. Harvus had to hunch beneath the low timbers, his height and wings made useless by the ceiling pressing down over him.

The rats attacked from beneath the water and behind the spoiled sacks. Teeth found scale, cloth, and skin. One fastened on and had to be torn loose. Another climbed a leaning board and launched itself from above. A third vanished under the surface, only to burst up between their legs in a spray of black water and rotten grain.

The three fought shoulder to shoulder because the bilges gave them no other choice.

Rak held the narrow center whenever the water allowed him footing. Sho struck at whatever broke the surface, fast enough to catch movement before it disappeared again. Harvus braced himself against the beams and drove the creatures away from the others whenever they tried to swarm through the confined space.

The fight filled the bilges with noise. Water slapped against wood. Claws scraped over boards. Bodies struck the low supports. The rats shrieked, the ship groaned around them, and the heat made every breath feel stolen.

Above, the Wormwood continued its ordinary labor.

Below, the three new hands killed the infestation one rat at a time.

The struggle was ugly and close. There were no clean charges, no open ground, and no room for anyone to look heroic. They fought in stagnant water with blood running into it, slipping on spoiled barley while the remaining rats lunged from beneath the surface.

Still, they held together.

One rat died against the boards. Another thrashed itself still in the water. A third was caught as it tried to scramble back into the broken sacks. The last two attacked almost together, desperate now that the nest had become a killing ground instead of a refuge.

When the final rat stopped moving, six bodies lay among the ruined grain.

For a moment, none of the three climbed out.

They stood in the sweltering dark, bitten, bloodied, and coated in bilge water. The rats were dead, but the place still belonged to rot, heat, and the slow movement of the sea beneath the hull.

Then they made their way back up.

The passage into the middle hold felt almost cool after the bilges. Crew members turned as they passed. Rak, Sho, and Harvus left dark water behind them with every step. Blood showed through torn cloth. The smell arrived before they did.

Plugg looked past their wounds to the work completed. Six rats were dead. The bilges were safe enough to ignore again.

Scourge watched all three return.

Yesterday, Jack had climbed out frightened and bitten. Today, the officers had sent three troublemakers into the same dark to be useful, punished, or disposed of.

They came back carrying proof that Jack had been right.

They also came back together.
