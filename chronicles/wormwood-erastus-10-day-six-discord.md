**Erastus 10, 4712 AR**  
**The Beast Plugg Made**  
*The Wormwood*

The sixth day passed beneath the bell, the rigging, and the ordinary demands of the Wormwood.

Work filled the hours. Sailors hauled, climbed, scrubbed, cooked, cursed, and carried. By evening the deck had given back enough heat for the crew to crowd together after the meal, bowls in hand, looking for whatever distraction might keep the next watch from arriving too quickly.

Plugg gave them one.

He had Owlbear Hartshorn brought up from the middle hold.

Owlbear had to turn his shoulders to clear the hatch. Once upright, he stood above most of the gathered crew, broad through the chest and heavy enough that the deck seemed built on a smaller scale around him. His arms hung thick at his sides, ending in fists that looked made for breaking doors. Tar still marked his skin in dark patches. Feathers clung where the crew had made sport of him before, remnants of a joke that had lasted longer on his body than in anyone else's memory.

The laughter began before he reached the center of the deck.

Owlbear answered it the way he had learned to. He grinned too widely, lurched his weight from foot to foot, and played at being the beast they expected. A few sailors shouted at him. Others called wagers across the ring forming near the mast. The noise rose whenever he performed for them and dipped whenever he looked uncertain.

Plugg watched from the edge of it all.

This was not a contest arranged for fairness. It was an evening's entertainment built from a captive man and whichever new hand Plugg wanted tested in front of the crew.

He chose Sho.

At first, she saw what Plugg wanted her to see.

Owlbear was his brute. His kept fighter. Another body Plugg could point at someone when threats and lashes were no longer enough. Sho had already spent days watching cruelty pass for authority aboard the Wormwood. Stepping into the ring felt like a chance to answer some of it with her own hands.

If she could put Owlbear down, Plugg would have to watch.

The rules were simple enough. Fists and wrestling. No weapons. No magic. Last one standing.

Sho entered the circle.

The crew tightened around them. Owlbear's shadow reached her before his hands did, and the difference between them became part of the spectacle. He had the mass to crush through a guard or carry someone bodily across the deck. Sho had space, speed, and whatever she could read before he closed it.

Owlbear came forward with force enough to make the deck feel smaller. The boards answered under his first step. His opening swing moved all of that weight at once, shoulder, chest, and fist driving through the place where Sho had been. She slipped away before it landed. The next blow followed more slowly, wide and heavy, striking empty air.

He was strong enough to end the fight quickly if he caught her.

He was also blind in one eye.

Sho noticed the turn of his head, the fraction of delay whenever she moved across that side, and the way he tried to compensate by swinging farther than he needed. She began to work around it.

A feint drew him forward. A quick change of angle left him striking past her. Sho stepped into the opening, landed cleanly, and moved before his hands could close around her.

The crowd shouted approval.

Owlbear laughed because they laughed.

He charged again.

Sho kept the fight controlled. She gave him movement to follow, then took it away. She attacked from the side he could not see and made him turn until his size became weight he had to drag after her. Every change of direction forced his whole body to follow, boots grinding against the boards while his shoulders came around a heartbeat too late. When he reached for her, she was already elsewhere. When he planted his feet to strike, she broke his balance before the blow could gather power.

This was not the bilges. There was room here. Light. Air. A threat she could read.

Sho did not panic.

She dismantled him.

The longer the fight continued, the less Owlbear looked like Plugg's weapon.

Each time he missed, the crew laughed harder. Each time Sho struck him, he searched the ring with his good eye. More than once, that eye went past her to Plugg.

He did not look to Plugg for encouragement.

He looked to see what would happen if he failed.

Sho saw it.

She saw the tar and feathers for what they were. She heard the foolish shouts he gave the crew and understood that he had learned which noises earned laughter instead of anger. The great brute in front of her was dangerous, but the danger had been trained, displayed, and aimed by someone standing safely outside the ring.

Owlbear was not one of Plugg's men.

He was one of Plugg's prisoners.

The fight had already turned. Sho could still hit him. She could drive him down, give the crew the ending it wanted, and show Plugg that his beast had failed.

Instead, when Owlbear could no longer press the attack, Sho stopped advancing.

The space between them opened.

The crew waited for her to close it.

She did not.

Owlbear remained where he was, breathing hard, bruised and unsteady. His chest rose and fell like a bellows, and his heavy arms hung lower with every breath. Sho lowered her hands enough to make the choice plain. She had beaten him. She was not going to keep hurting him because Plugg had gathered an audience.

The noise around the ring faltered.

Plugg reacted.

Owlbear saw it.

Then he looked back at Sho.

Something changed in that look. The confusion remained, but the fear shifted away from her. He understood at least this much: she could have continued, and she had chosen not to.

The fight ended without the cruelty Plugg had arranged it to produce.

The crew broke apart slowly. Some laughed again because they were expected to. Some argued over wagers. Others watched Sho and Owlbear with the wary attention the Wormwood gave anything that had not gone according to plan.

When Owlbear was taken below, he kept looking back at Sho.

She had entered the ring ready to put down one of Plugg's brutes.

She left it knowing she had met another captive.
