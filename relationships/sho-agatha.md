# Sho Astor and Agatha Dreamriver

## Document Status

- Type: Relationship record
- Maturity: Draft, seeded from Sho player-memory answer
- Scope: Sho's pre-Wormwood emotional anchor, future objective, and survival promise
- Source basis: `evidence/player-memory/sho-astor-agatha-dreamriver-player-answer.md` plus existing Sho dossier backstory notes
- Canon status: Sho-character truth / player memory. Agatha's actual response remains unplayed and unresolved unless GM truth or future play establishes it.

## Summary

Agatha Dreamriver is one of Sho Astor's central emotional anchors.

To Sho, Agatha represents kindness, freedom, beauty, strength, and the proof that Sho could have a future beyond the monastery.

Before Agatha, Sho expected to remain at the monastery forever. She did not have real goals and had not truly internalized that she was allowed to have goals.

Meeting Agatha changed that.

## What Agatha Represented

Agatha showed Sho that someone could be beautiful and strong, kind and free, inspiring instead of stifling.

This mattered because Sho's monastic upbringing had disciplined her heavily and left little room for softness, desire, self-directed ambition, or ordinary joy.

Agatha opened Sho to the possibilities of the world.

She means the world to Sho.

## Realization After Absence

Sho did not fully understand what she felt until after Agatha had left Jalmeray.

The absence of Agatha's kindness, beauty, freedom, and possibility made Sho realize how much she needed those feelings in her life.

That absence also helped Sho realize that she needed to go out into the world, see it, experience it, learn from it, and become someone with her own knowledge rather than only hearing about the world secondhand.

## Sho's Desired Future

Sho absolutely wanted to find Agatha again.

Sho wanted to become more learned and impress Agatha with her own knowledge of the world.

She wanted to be with Agatha moving forward and see the world together.

This is not only a romantic or emotional longing. It is also a future-image: Sho as someone free, knowledgeable, and worthy of standing beside the person who first made the world seem possible.

## Survival Anchor

Agatha gives Sho a focus that helps direct her actions.

During the Wormwood and Man's Promise ordeals, Agatha remained a goal Sho could hope to reach after escape.

Sho made a promise to herself that she would see Agatha again.

That promise remains active: come hell or high water, Sho still wants to meet her.

## Shame After the Man's Promise

Sho worries that Agatha might be disgusted by what Sho has done, especially after the Man's Promise.

This fear belongs to Sho's guilt and shame. It should not be treated as Agatha's actual judgment unless Agatha appears or GM truth establishes it.

Sho believes Agatha would be taken aback and unhappy if she knew everything, but might eventually understand why Sho took the actions she did, knowing how dangerous the sea can be.

## Relationship Function

Agatha functions as:

- a symbol of kindness and freedom,
- a contrast to the monastery's stifling discipline,
- the origin of Sho's belief that she could have a life beyond obedience,
- a survival anchor during captivity,
- a future objective after escape,
- and a moral mirror for Sho's fear that she has become someone Agatha could not accept.

## What Not To Assume

Do not assume Agatha's actual reaction to Sho's actions.

Do not reduce Agatha to romance only.

Do not treat Sho's fear of disgust as fact.

Do not treat Sho's desire to see Agatha again as abandoned after the Man's Promise. The opposite is true: the promise remains active.
