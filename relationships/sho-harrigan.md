# Sho Astor and Barnabas Harrigan

## Document Status

- Type: Relationship / perception record
- Maturity: Draft, seeded from Man's Promise reward evidence and Sho player answers
- Scope: Sho's warning to Harrigan, captain's reward, confused respect, guilt, and distinction between Harrigan and Plugg / Scourge
- Source basis: Sho player answers relayed by GM; recovered Man's Promise battle and reward reconstruction
- Spoiler scope: Character-private / GM-facing until revealed in play

## Summary

Sho Astor does not view Barnabas Harrigan as safe or good, but she also does not place him in the same immediate moral category as Mr. Plugg and Master Scourge.

Sho warned Harrigan during the Man's Promise battle on instinct. She saw an attack about to happen and acted before thinking through what it meant. Part of that impulse may also have been a refusal to allow a dishonorable murder.

Harrigan's later reward left Sho confused, uncomfortable, and guilty rather than simply proud.

## The Warning

Sho does not fully know why she warned Harrigan.

The best current reconstruction is instinct: she saw the attack forming and warned him.

Sho often acts before considering consequences, and this was one of those moments. She did not realize what the warning would mean until afterward.

A refusal to allow a dishonorable killing may also belong in the interpretation, but it should not be overstated as a calculated moral position.

## The Reward

Harrigan's reward created confusion and discomfort.

Sho did not fully grasp how much her own life was on the line during the Man's Promise fight. Afterward, she was discomforted that her life had been spared or improved at the expense of innocent people's lives.

She reads the reward as luck mixed with guilt.

## The Pendant / Captain's Amulet

Sho does not know what to do with the pendant or how to feel about it.

She does not want to get rid of it, but her feelings are very confused.

The item should not be treated as a clean trophy. It is a morally complicated object tied to survival, recognition, and the deaths of innocent people.

## Distinction From Plugg and Scourge

Sho sees a distinction between Harrigan and Plugg / Scourge.

Harrigan may need to be taken down for his actions at some point.

Plugg and Scourge, by contrast, need to be taken out as soon as possible in Sho's mind.

The distinction is not absolution. It is priority, category, and moral readability. Plugg and Scourge delight in malice. Harrigan is cruel and dangerous, but his competence and reward logic make him harder for Sho to categorize.
