# Mirei and Sho Astor

## Document Status

- Type: Relationship record
- Maturity: Draft, seeded from recovered disguise-discovery whispers, Sho player memory, and Sho player disguise answers
- Scope: Early Wormwood suspicion, disguise truth, secrecy, and later trust/confiding thread

## Summary

Mirei was the first recovered player character to notice that Sho Astor was not what she appeared to be aboard the Wormwood.

On Erastus 7 / Wormwood Day 3, Mirei noticed through deception / Bluff context that something about Sho was off. By the following night, the GM clarified that Mirei had noticed the previous day that Sho was actually a woman.

Sho later remembered Mirei as the person who noticed early and became someone Sho confided in.

The Sho player has confirmed that once Mirei showed she could and would keep the secret, the initial tension quickly became trust.

## Sho's Disguise Meaning

Sho's male presentation / disguise had several overlapping causes:

- Safety: people tended to mess with Sho less or exploit her lack of worldly knowledge less when she presented as masculine.
- Monastery habit: Sho's monastery used very gender-neutral clothing, focused more on function than presentation.
- Agatha: Sho did not want to dress in anything fancy for anyone other than Agatha when she saw her again.
- Control: even after partial exposure, Sho would still try to maintain the presentation as a way to preserve some control.

This should not be written as only fear, only shame, or only a tactical disguise. It is a layered survival habit and personal boundary.

## Discovery Sequence

### Initial Suspicion

Recovered GM whispers show that Mirei sensed something was wrong with Sho's presentation.

> "You sensed that something isn't quite what it appears with Chas"

The GM asked whether Mirei wanted to hide that she noticed.

Mirei initially chose not to hide the suspicion:

> "no, I dont want to hide. Already on the path to asking him what the f is up"

### Exact Realization

The next night, Mirei asked whether she got a new check while talking with Sho during dinner, or whether the prior check still stood.

The GM clarified:

> "you noticed that chas is actually a woman.... are you trying to hide that you noticed. YESTERDAY."

The GM further said:

> "she is lying to you, clearly."

Mirei then chose to hide that she had figured out the exact truth:

> "Yes, I want to hide that I figured exactly out what was up. Chas knows I am suspicious at this point."

## Trust Beat

The Sho player confirmed that the discovery created tension at first, but that it quickly turned into trust when Mirei proved she could and would keep the secret.

Sho directly asked Mirei whether she would keep it secret.

Mirei agreed.

This is the key transformation in the relationship: Mirei's knowledge was dangerous at first, then became proof that she could be trusted with something that mattered.

## Knowledge Boundaries

- None of the player characters knew Sho's disguise truth at first.
- Mirei was the first recovered PC to notice and then become a secret-keeper.
- Sho directly asked Mirei to keep the secret, and Mirei agreed.
- Select Wormwood crew may have known or suspected if they searched Sho's belongings during press-ganging.
- The exact press-gangers who may have known are unresolved.
- If directly confronted, Sho would likely admit who and what she is, but she would still prefer to maintain the presentation for control.

## Relationship Meaning

This creates a layered early trust problem:

- Mirei notices that Sho is hiding something.
- Sho knows Mirei is suspicious.
- Mirei eventually knows the exact truth but chooses to hide that she knows.
- Sho then asks Mirei to keep the secret.
- Mirei keeps it.
- Trust forms because Mirei protects the secret rather than using it.

This should not be treated as a simple reveal. It is a slow recognition / suspicion / concealment / request / trust sequence.

## Campaign Function

This relationship matters because both characters are navigating secrecy under coercive shipboard power.

Sho's disguise is a survival boundary and a personal boundary tied to monastery habit and Agatha.

Mirei's knowledge of the disguise makes her dangerous to Sho if mishandled, but safe once she proves trustworthy.

Later, Mirei's own Plugg pressure and betrayal assignment create a mirror: Sho once trusted Mirei with a dangerous secret, and Mirei later feared disappointing Sho when forced into her own dangerous secret.
