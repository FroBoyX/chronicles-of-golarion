# Pirate Punishments

## Document Status

- Type: Culture / operating guide
- Maturity: Draft
- Scope: Shipboard punishment as campaign culture and authority tool

## Purpose

This document records how punishment functions aboard pirate ships in Chronicles of Golarion.

Punishment is not only damage. It is public theater, discipline, fear, legitimacy, and sometimes cruelty.

## Core Principle

On a ship, punishment is public because ships are small societies.

The target is one sailor, but the message is for everyone.

## Discipline Versus Cruelty

The campaign should distinguish discipline from cruelty.

Discipline can preserve the ship.

Cruelty preserves only the authority of the cruel.

Plugg and Scourge blur this line deliberately.

A future crew must decide whether it can maintain order without reproducing the Wormwood.

## Common Punishments

### Rope Bash

A minor but humiliating nonlethal punishment.

Usually used for lesser failures or as informal correction.

### Lash

A whip punishment, usually public.

The lash can be used for failed work, insolence, refusal, or discipline display.

### Cat-o'-Nine-Tails

A harsher lash associated with more severe shipboard punishment.

On the Man's Promise, Plugg escalates discipline by making more crimes punishable with the cat rather than the ordinary whip.

This shift is one sign that the Man's Promise under Plugg is harsher than the Wormwood, not freer.

### Sweatbox / Hot-Box

A confinement punishment in a cramped box, often exposed to heat and public view.

Sho is currently believed locked in the hot-box aboard the Man's Promise.

This makes the punishment a live social scene rather than only a condition.

### Keelhauling

A usually lethal punishment in which the victim is dragged under the ship.

Jakes Magpie's keelhauling aboard the Wormwood taught the players and PCs that shipboard punishment could kill.

Keelhauling is one of the clearest signs that the ship's law is real, not performative.

## Campaign Punishment Events

### Jakes Magpie

Jakes was keelhauled early aboard the Wormwood for theft from the quartermaster's store.

His death established the stakes of shipboard law.

### Rum Refusal and Lashings

Punishments around rum refusal helped reveal that rum is not only recreation. It is part of control.

### Sho and Scourge

Sho repeatedly drew punishment through defiance.

Her current hot-box state aboard the Man's Promise continues that line.

### Harvus and the Wing

Scourge's damage to Harvus' wing was not legitimate punishment. It was mutilation and control.

This distinction matters to Harvus' revenge.

### Plugg's Man's Promise Regime

Plugg claims the rules are the same on the Man's Promise but makes discipline harsher.

This is a legitimacy failure: he imitates command without earning it.

## Punishment as Political Signal

When someone is punished, ask:

- Who ordered it?
- Who carried it out?
- Who watched?
- Who objected?
- Who benefited?
- Was the punishment necessary for ship survival?
- Was it personal?
- Did it increase or reduce the punisher's legitimacy?

## Post-Mutiny Problem

If Plugg and Scourge fall, the crew must still answer:

- What replaces Bloody Hour?
- Are lashings still used?
- Is keelhauling ever acceptable?
- Who decides guilt?
- What happens to captured enemies?
- What happens to Plugg's loyalists?
- What happens to crew who refuse command?

The absence of punishment is not automatically freedom.

A ship without order may become uncrewable.
