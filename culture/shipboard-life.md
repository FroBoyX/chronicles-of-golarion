# Shipboard Life

## Document Status

- Type: Culture / operating guide
- Maturity: Draft
- Scope: Daily life aboard pirate ships as used by Chronicles of Golarion

## Purpose

This document explains the lived rhythm of shipboard life that Book One teaches through play.

It is not primarily a mechanics document.

It exists to preserve the culture that made the Wormwood and Man's Promise feel real.

## Core Principle

A pirate ship is not a static dungeon.

It is a moving workplace, prison, military unit, tavern, marketplace, court, and powder keg.

People are always watching. People are always talking. People are always tired.

## Daily Rhythm

A standard day aboard a coercive pirate vessel follows a recognizable pattern:

1. Dawn bell.
2. Breakfast or ship's biscuit.
3. Work assignments.
4. Hard labor.
5. Secret or semi-secret social action while working.
6. Bloody Hour.
7. Evening meal.
8. Rum ration.
9. Gambling, entertainment, influence, theft, plotting, or sleep.
10. Middle watch for those willing to risk exhaustion.

The repetition is the point.

The crew is shaped by routine before it is shaped by battle.

## Work

Work is survival.

A ship that does not work does not sail.

Common work categories:

- rigging repair,
- sail handling,
- rope work,
- lookout,
- swabbing decks,
- bilge pumping,
- rat catching,
- repairs,
- fishing,
- hunting food,
- cooking,
- hauling stores.

The labor is not background color. It determines fatigue, resentment, competence, and social contact.

## Punishment

Punishment is public because the lesson is meant for the crew, not only the victim.

Punishment establishes who has power and how far that power can go before it becomes illegitimate.

On the Wormwood, punishment teaches fear.

On the Man's Promise, Plugg and Scourge push punishment toward overt tyranny.

## Rum

Rum is recreation, addiction, payment, sedation, and control.

A rum ration can make a crew easier to manage, harder to organize, and more dependent on the ship's routine.

Refusing, spilling, hiding, or trading rum can become a political act if punishment is attached.

## Influence

Influence aboard ship is not optional.

Friends can:

- cover mistakes,
- share rumors,
- lend gear,
- help with work,
- warn of danger,
- vote or fight during mutiny,
- or simply keep someone sane.

Enemies can:

- report theft,
- arrange ambushes,
- poison reputations,
- join Plugg or Scourge,
- or decide the numbers in a fight.

## Privacy

Privacy is rare.

The lower crew sleeps together. People overhear, spy, gossip, and interpret everything.

A secret aboard ship is not safe because it is hidden. It is safe only if no one has reason to profit from revealing it.

## Theft

Theft is common.

The crime is often getting caught, stealing from the wrong person, or embarrassing authority.

The same act may be tolerated, punished, or celebrated depending on target and timing.

## Entertainment

Entertainment relieves pressure and builds reputation.

Songs, gambling, stories, arm wrestling, drinking games, and performance can all alter shipboard relationships.

A performer can become valuable because morale is valuable.

This matters especially for characters like Mirei, Sandara, Conchobhar, and Rosie.

## Discipline Versus Cruelty

Discipline is necessary aboard a ship.

Cruelty is not.

One of Book One's central questions is whether the crew can distinguish discipline from tyranny.

Scourge corrupts discipline into sadism.

Plugg treats discipline as proof of command.

A future captain or council must answer what discipline means without reproducing the Wormwood.

## Legitimacy

A pirate captain's authority can come from multiple sources:

- force,
- competence,
- crew consent,
- wealth,
- fear,
- reputation,
- divine favor,
- success,
- custom,
- or emergency necessity.

These sources can conflict.

The end of Book One should preserve that conflict rather than flattening captaincy into a title.

## The Party Problem

The PCs were press-ganged together.

That does not automatically make them a crew.

They must earn trust from each other, and the NPCs must decide whether they are worth following.

The ship's future depends on that earned legitimacy.

## Using This Document in Play

When uncertain how shipboard culture should respond, ask:

- Who saw it?
- Who benefits from talking?
- Who has authority to punish?
- Who has practical leverage?
- Does the action help the ship survive?
- Does it humiliate someone dangerous?
- Does it create a story worth retelling?
