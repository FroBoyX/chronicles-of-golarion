# Plunder and Infamy

## Document Status

- Type: Culture / campaign-system guide
- Maturity: Draft
- Scope: Reputation, plunder, and post-Book-One pirate identity

## Purpose

This document records how plunder and reputation should function in Chronicles of Golarion.

The published Adventure Path treats Plunder and Infamy as subsystems.

The Chronicle should treat them as culture first and mechanics second.

## Core Principle

Pirates do not live by gold alone.

They need cargo, reputation, ports, stories, proof, fear, generosity, and witnesses.

## Plunder

Plunder represents useful but not always individually interesting cargo:

- trade goods,
- foodstuffs,
- spices,
- sailcloth,
- timber,
- weapons,
- tools,
- valuables,
- and other sellable or distributable goods.

In fiction, plunder is what a crew can divide, sell, boast about, or spend to build loyalty.

## Infamy

Infamy represents the spread of stories.

A pirate crew becomes infamous when people hear what it has done and believe the story enough to change behavior.

Infamy requires:

- a deed,
- a storyteller,
- an audience,
- and often proof.

## Disrepute

Disrepute is reputation that can be spent.

A feared or admired crew can make demands, receive favors, intimidate merchants, recruit sailors, or secure services more easily.

A crew with no reputation has to pay, beg, threaten directly, or prove itself again.

## Why This Matters After Bonewrack

If the PCs take, contest, abandon, or transform the Man's Promise, they will need a story.

Possible stories:

- escaped press-ganged sailors overthrow tyrants,
- mutineers steal Harrigan's prize,
- Harvus kills the Whale of Bonewrack,
- Plugg loses control of his own prize crew,
- Man's Promise survivors turn on pirate oppressors,
- a new crew forms by council instead of command,
- or no crew forms and the ship becomes a ghost prize.

Each story creates different reputation.

## Harvus and the First Legend

The Rescue of Sandara and the killing of the Whale may be the campaign's first true legend.

It can become Infamy, honor, religious testimony, tavern exaggeration, or a private story depending on who tells it.

The story's effect depends on framing.

## Story Control

Ask:

- Who tells the story first?
- Where is it told?
- Does Sandara confirm it?
- Does Harvus deny or avoid it?
- Does Rak claim the spear's role?
- Does Mirei get remembered?
- Does the Brinebrood Queen become part of the tale?
- Is the story told as rescue, monster-slaying, or mutiny propaganda?

## Plunder as Crew Glue

After the island, plunder may matter less as wealth and more as crew legitimacy.

A new captain or council must answer:

- Who gets paid?
- What happens to Plugg's share?
- Are Man's Promise survivors compensated?
- Are hostile crew allowed shares?
- Does the crew prioritize repair over reward?
- Does the ship have enough stores to sail?

## Reputation Is Conditional

The same act can produce different reputations.

Killing the Whale may be:

- heroic to Sandara,
- terrifying to grindylows,
- irrelevant to merchants,
- interesting to Besmarans,
- threatening to Harrigan,
- and enraging to the Brinebrood Queen.

## Open Questions

- Will the campaign continue using formal Plunder and Infamy scores?
- If converting to 5e, should these remain as narrative clocks/resources?
- Who is the crew's first storyteller?
- What port first hears the Bonewrack story?
- Does Harrigan hear a true version or a distorted one?
- Does the Brinebrood Queen's revenge begin as counter-legend?
